## 7. Full page content

### Why the shoulder needs precision

The shoulder is one of the most mobile joints in the body — and one of the most complex. Its small structures (subacromial bursa, rotator cuff tendons, biceps tendon sheath, AC joint) sit close to nerves and blood vessels.

This is the joint where **image-guided injections shine the most**: accurate placement transforms outcomes.

> Pair this page with [Ultrasound-Guided Orthopedic Injections](/ultrasound-guided-orthopedic-injections) for the technical details.

---

### Common shoulder problems we treat

- **Rotator cuff tendinopathy** — overuse pain in cuff tendons (supraspinatus most common)
- **Subacromial bursitis** — painful inflammation of the bursa beneath the acromion
- **Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder)** — progressive stiffness and pain
- **AC joint pain** — pain at the top of the shoulder
- **Biceps tendinopathy** — pain at the front of the shoulder
- **Calcific tendinopathy** — calcium deposits in cuff tendons (image-guided needling/aspiration may help)
- **Selected partial cuff tears** — non-operatively managed in many patients
- **Post-surgery persistent pain** — case-by-case evaluation

---

### Treatment ladder

**Step 1 — Diagnosis**
History + physical exam + imaging (X-ray for joint, ultrasound for soft tissue, MRI in selected cases).

**Step 2 — Foundation rehab**
Targeted physiotherapy (cuff strengthening, scapular control) is essential and often sufficient for milder cases.

**Step 3 — Image-guided injections**
- **Corticosteroid + local anaesthetic** — for selected cases of acute, painful inflammation (e.g., subacromial bursitis, frozen shoulder), used judiciously
- **Hydrodilatation** — for frozen shoulder, an image-guided procedure that distends the joint capsule
- **GFC / regenerative injections** — for chronic tendinopathy and selected partial tears
- **Aspiration** — for calcific tendinopathy

**Step 4 — Combination protocols**
Injection + structured rehab almost always outperforms either alone.

**Step 5 — Surgical referral**
Reserved for full-thickness cuff tears, structural problems, or shoulders that fail comprehensive non-surgical care.

### Recovery and aftercare

- **24–72 hours:** Mild soreness is common.
- **Avoid heavy overhead lifting** for 3–5 days unless told otherwise.
- **Begin or continue physiotherapy** as advised.
- **Onset of relief:** Steroid relief within days; regenerative effect (GFC) builds over 2–6 weeks.

## 8. FAQ section

**Q1. What causes most shoulder pain?**
The most common causes are rotator cuff tendinopathy, subacromial bursitis, frozen shoulder, and AC joint pain.

**Q2. Is ultrasound guidance necessary for shoulder injections?**
Strongly preferred. The shoulder has many small targets close to delicate structures — image guidance dramatically improves accuracy.

**Q3. Are steroid injections safe for the shoulder?**
Yes, when used selectively and not too frequently. Repeated steroid injections are avoided for chronic tendinopathy.

**Q4. Can frozen shoulder be treated without surgery?**
Yes — most cases respond to a combination of rehab, image-guided injection, and time. Surgery is rarely needed.

**Q5. How many shoulder injections will I need?**
Often 1–2 for steroid; 1–3 for GFC. Personalised by case.

**Q6. Is GFC better than steroid for the rotator cuff?**
For chronic tendinopathy, GFC is often preferred over repeated steroid use.

**Q7. Will I be able to drive after?**
Most patients can. We recommend a short rest before leaving the clinic.

**Q8. Will rehab still be needed?**
Yes. Rehab is the foundation of shoulder recovery.

**Q9. Are partial cuff tears always surgical?**
Many partial tears can be managed non-operatively, especially in older patients. Each case is individualised.
