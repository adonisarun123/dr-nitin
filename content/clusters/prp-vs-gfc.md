### Introduction

If you have been researching non-surgical treatments for knee pain, tendinopathy, or a sports injury, you have probably come across two names: **PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma)** and **GFC (Growth Factor Concentrate)**. Both are regenerative injection therapies prepared from your own blood. Both aim to reduce pain and support tissue health. So — what's actually different, and which one might suit you?

Here's a straightforward, patient-friendly explanation from Dr. Nitin N Sunku.

> Looking for the bigger picture? Start with [Non-Surgical Knee Pain Treatment](/non-surgical-knee-pain-treatment) and [What Is Regenerative Medicine?](/what-is-regenerative-medicine).

---

### What is PRP?

PRP, or **Platelet-Rich Plasma**, is prepared by drawing a small amount of your blood and spinning it in a centrifuge to concentrate the platelets. Platelets carry growth factors that play a role in inflammation modulation and tissue repair.

A typical PRP preparation contains:

- A higher-than-baseline concentration of platelets
- Plasma (the liquid component of blood)
- Often some white blood cells (WBCs) and a small number of red blood cells (RBCs), depending on the preparation kit

PRP has been used in orthopedics, sports medicine, and aesthetics for many years.

---

### What is GFC?

**GFC (Growth Factor Concentrate)** uses the same starting material — your own blood — but goes further. The processing aims to **release and isolate the growth factors directly**, with reduced cellular debris (fewer red and white blood cells), giving a cleaner, more standardised dose of growth factor signalling molecules.

In effect, GFC is often described as an **evolved, more refined version of PRP**, focused specifically on the active healing signals.

---

### Side-by-side comparison

| Aspect | PRP | GFC |
|---|---|---|
| Source | Your own blood | Your own blood |
| Main active component | Platelets + growth factors they release | Concentrated **growth factors** |
| Cellular debris | More | Less |
| Standardisation of dose | Variable | More standardised |
| Injection texture | Often thicker | Typically clearer & smoother |
| Common indications | Mild-to-moderate OA, tendinopathy, sports injuries | Same, with preference where higher growth-factor purity is desired |
| Typical sessions | 1–3 | 1–3 |
| Safety | High (autologous) | High (autologous) |

> Both PRP and GFC are autologous (made from you), so allergic reactions are rare. Side effects, when present, are usually short-term soreness or swelling.

---

### Which one is "better"?

There is no universally better option — only the better fit for *your* situation. Generally:

- **GFC may be preferred** when:
 - A more standardised, growth-factor-rich injection is desired
 - The target tissue (e.g., a small tendon) needs a cleaner injectate
 - The patient has had limited benefit from a previous PRP
- **PRP may still be appropriate** when:
 - The clinical situation is well suited to platelet-rich preparations
 - Specific indications or patient preferences favour it
 - It is the option available with the team treating the patient

In Dr. Nitin N Sunku's practice, GFC is often the regenerative option of choice for **mild-to-moderate knee OA, patellar tendinopathy, and select shoulder/elbow tendinopathies**, while PRP retains a role in selected cases.

> Decision is always individualised after examination, imaging review, and your goals.

---

### What both PRP and GFC are NOT

- They do **not** regrow lost cartilage to a healthy joint.
- They are **not** a guaranteed cure for OA.
- They will **not** reverse a severe, end-stage joint.
- They are **not** a substitute for physiotherapy, weight management, and lifestyle care.

They are **biological tools** — when used in the right patient with the right plan, they can reduce pain, improve function, and help delay surgery in suitable cases.

---

### How does Dr. Nitin N Sunku decide PRP vs GFC for you?

The decision usually rests on:

1. **Diagnosis & severity** — OA grade, tendon condition, presence of mechanical issues
2. **Target tissue** — small/deep targets often benefit from cleaner GFC
3. **Imaging findings** — joint space, swelling, tendon structure
4. **Goals** — return to sport vs daily comfort vs surgery delay
5. **Prior treatments** — what you have already tried and how you responded
6. **Logistics** — number of sessions, cost, follow-up plan

### Realistic expectations

- Onset of relief is usually gradual — 2–4 weeks for early benefit, 6–12 weeks for peak.
- Some patients need a planned series of 2–3 sessions.

- Combination with [HA injections](/hyaluronic-acid-injection-treatment) is sometimes ideal.

## 8. FAQ section

**Q1. Are PRP and GFC the same thing?**
No. They share the same starting material (your blood) but differ in processing and end product. GFC offers a more refined, growth-factor-focused output.

**Q2. Is GFC stronger than PRP?**
GFC concentrates growth factors more specifically and reduces cellular debris. Whether this is "stronger" depends on the indication.

**Q3. Which has more side effects?**
Both have a strong safety profile because they are autologous. Side effects are usually limited to short-term soreness or swelling.

**Q4. Can I switch from PRP to GFC if PRP didn't work?**
Yes — many patients with limited PRP response are reassessed and considered for GFC. The decision depends on examination and imaging.

**Q5. Which is better for knee OA?**
Both can help in mild-to-moderate OA. GFC is often preferred where a cleaner, more standardised growth-factor delivery is desired.

**Q6. Which is better for tendinopathy?**
For small or deep tendons, GFC delivered under ultrasound guidance is frequently a strong choice. PRP remains valid in many cases.

**Q7. How many sessions are typically needed?**
1–3 for both, depending on severity and response.

**Q8. Are PRP or GFC covered by insurance?**
Coverage varies. The clinic team will help you check your policy.

**Q9. Can I combine PRP/GFC with hyaluronic acid?**
Yes. Combination protocols are common for moderate OA.

**Q10. Are these treatments safe for diabetics or older adults?**
Generally yes, when overall health is reasonably stable. Each case is individualised.

**Q11. How soon will I feel relief?**
Most patients begin noticing improvement around 2–4 weeks; peak relief usually 6–12 weeks.

**Q12. Can I drive and work after the procedure?**
Most patients drive home and return to office work the next day.

**Q13. Should I avoid painkillers afterwards?**
Avoid NSAIDs for the period your doctor advises, as they may blunt the regenerative response. Paracetamol is usually fine.

**Q14. How is the right option chosen for me?**
After clinical examination, imaging review, and a discussion of your goals.
