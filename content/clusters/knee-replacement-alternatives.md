## 7. Full page content

### When patients come to us before knee replacement

Many patients arrive at our clinics carrying an MRI report and a recommendation: "knee replacement." Some are convinced they have no choice. Others are looking for a second opinion. Almost all are scared.

Our message is simple:

- **Knee replacement is sometimes the right answer.** When OA is severe and life is being seriously compromised, modern joint replacement transforms lives.
- **But knee replacement is not always the *next* answer.** Many patients still have meaningful, real alternatives — and deserve to hear about them honestly.

This page lays out those alternatives clearly.

> Want the full picture first? Read [Non-Surgical Knee Pain Treatment](/non-surgical-knee-pain-treatment) and [Osteoarthritis Knee Treatment](/osteoarthritis-knee-treatment).

---

### Alternative 1 — Foundation care, done properly

Most "knee replacement candidates" have never been given a structured non-surgical plan. A genuine attempt at non-surgical care includes:

- **Supervised physiotherapy** for at least 8–12 weeks
- **Quadriceps and hip strengthening**
- **Weight reduction** of 5–10% if BMI is high
- **Activity coaching** (stair use, walking surface, footwear)
- **Symptomatic medication** when appropriate, used strategically rather than chronically

Patients who actually complete this foundation often experience more relief than they expected — sometimes enough to defer surgery for years.

### Alternative 2 — Hyaluronic Acid (HA) injections

HA viscosupplementation can restore some of the lubrication and cushioning lost in OA. Many mild-to-moderate OA patients gain meaningful comfort for several months at a time and can postpone surgery.

➡ [Hyaluronic Acid Injection Treatment](/hyaluronic-acid-injection-treatment)

---

### Alternative 3 — GFC (Growth Factor Concentrate) therapy

GFC delivers a concentrated dose of growth factors derived from your own blood, supporting the joint's biological environment. Often paired with HA in moderate OA. Especially useful when imaging is "not yet end-stage" but pain is significant.

➡ [GFC Treatment for Knee Pain](/gfc-treatment-knee-pain) · [PRP vs GFC](/prp-vs-gfc)

---

### Alternative 4 — Ultrasound-guided precision injections

Many patients have had unsuccessful "blind" injections elsewhere. With image guidance, the medication actually reaches the right structure, often producing better and longer relief.

➡ [Ultrasound-Guided Orthopedic Injections](/ultrasound-guided-orthopedic-injections)

---

### Alternative 5 — Targeted partial procedures (when truly indicated)

In certain cases, a less invasive procedure — for example, addressing a meniscal issue or correcting alignment — may be more appropriate than a full knee replacement. These are considered carefully and case-by-case.

---

### When knee replacement *is* the right answer

We will tell you honestly if you are at that point. Replacement may be the better path when:

- OA is severe (Grade 4) with significant deformity
- Pain is constant, severe, and present at rest or at night
- Walking distance is severely limited
- Daily life is significantly impaired despite a real non-surgical trial
- Imaging shows mechanical issues that injections cannot fix

In such cases, we coordinate with experienced joint-replacement surgeons to ensure you get a great surgical outcome with full transparency.

## 8. FAQ section

**Q1. Are there real alternatives to knee replacement?**
Yes. For many patients with mild-to-moderate OA, structured non-surgical care, HA, GFC, and ultrasound-guided injections are real alternatives. End-stage OA may still need surgery.

**Q2. Can I avoid knee replacement permanently?**
For some patients, yes. For others, surgery may eventually be needed. An honest assessment is essential.

**Q3. How long can non-surgical care delay surgery?**
This varies — from months to many years — depending on OA grade, weight, lifestyle, and follow-up.

**Q4. Will exercise alone be enough?**
For mild OA, often yes. For moderate OA, exercise plus injections frequently outperforms either alone.

**Q5. Are HA and GFC injections better than surgery?**
They are different tools. Injections aim to manage symptoms and slow progression; surgery replaces a damaged joint. The right tool depends on severity.

**Q6. Can a second opinion really change my recommendation?**
Yes — different specialists weigh symptoms, function, and imaging differently. A second opinion gives you a fuller picture.

**Q7. Is bone-on-bone always surgical?**
Not always. Some bone-on-bone patients still respond meaningfully to non-surgical care for a useful period.

**Q8. What if I am overweight?**
Even modest weight loss (5–10%) can substantially reduce pain and improve outcomes from any treatment, surgical or otherwise.

**Q9. How do I prepare for a second opinion visit?**
Bring your X-ray and MRI files (digital is fine), prior reports, and a list of medications and prior treatments tried.
